The Origins of Mother’s Day

Mother’s Day, as we know it today, has its roots in ancient cultures. The Greeks and Romans held festivals to honor the mother goddesses Rhea and Cybele. In early Christian times, the fourth Sunday of Lent was known as Mothering Sunday, a day when people visited their “mother” church and gave gifts to their mothers. The modern Mother’s Day, however, can be traced back to the United States in the early 20th century. Anna Jarvis, a social activist, started campaigning for a day to honor mothers after her own mother’s death in 1905. Her efforts paid off when President Woodrow Wilson proclaimed the second Sunday of May as Mother’s Day in 1914.

Mother’s Day Around the World

Mother’s Day is celebrated in various ways around the world. In the United Kingdom, Mothering Sunday is still observed on the fourth Sunday of Lent. In Mexico, Mother’s Day is celebrated on May 10, regardless of the day of the week. In Thailand, Mother’s Day is celebrated on August 12, the birthday of Queen Sirikit, who is considered the mother of the nation.

How Mother’s Day is Celebrated

Mother’s Day is a day to show appreciation for mothers and mother figures. It is celebrated by giving gifts, sending cards or flowers, and spending time with loved ones. Many families also have special traditions, such as brunch or dinner together, or going on a family outing.

In conclusion, Mother’s Day has a rich history and has evolved over time. It is a day to honor and appreciate mothers and their contributions to society.
